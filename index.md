# Website improvement proposal for Andrew Sidford Architects

**Andrew Sidford Architects (ASA) has built a 30-year practice with award-winning work, an AIA-recognized jazz club, press features in the Boston Globe and Design New England, and a sustainability legacy as the first architecture firm to join 1% For the Planet — yet almost none of this story is told on asidfordarchitects.com.** The current Wix-based site functions as a basic digital business card with a photo grid, missing the strategic elements that turn websites into client-generating engines. This proposal benchmarks ASA against 14 leading architecture firm websites and provides a prioritized roadmap to transform the site into a tool that showcases the firm's caliber, converts visitors into leads, and positions ASA for its next chapter — including the NYC expansion announced in late 2025.

Research shows **84% of potential clients visit a firm's website before ever making contact**, and 9 out of 10 will rule out a firm based on that visit alone. With the top three Google results capturing 68% of all clicks, the gap between ASA's excellent work and its underwhelming web presence represents the firm's single largest marketing vulnerability.

---

## What Andrew Sidford Architects' website does today

The site at asidfordarchitects.com runs on **Wix** with a copyright date of 2021. It contains roughly 30 pages: a homepage, portfolio pages split into Commercial (10 projects) and Residential (13 projects), a Before & After section, a Details gallery, a Meet the Team page, a Contact page, and individual project pages. The navigation is functional: Home, About, Portfolio (dropdown), Meet the Team, News & Updates, and Contact.

**What's working.** The firm has strong raw materials. Professional photography from credited photographers (Vanderwarker, Karosis Auger) anchors the portfolio. Individual project pages like Jimmy's Jazz & Blues Club include compelling ~200-word narratives covering building history, design intent, community engagement, and accessibility features, accompanied by 24+ completed project photos, before-condition images, and architectural drawings. The Meet the Team page features **six team members with substantive bios and professional headshots**, highlighting Andrew Sidford's 30 years of experience, Cindy Schartman's Smithsonian portfolio, and the team's diverse credentials from RPI, UPenn, Catholic University, and NC State. The URL structure is clean and readable.

**What's missing is nearly everything that converts visitors.** The homepage has zero body copy — no headline, no tagline, no firm description, no value proposition. Visitors land on portfolio thumbnail links and an embedded Instagram feed where, at time of analysis, the most prominent content was a job posting seeking an architect. The firm's powerful differentiator — "For 25 years we have collaborated with our clients to create thoughtful architecture that matters" — appears only in the meta description and on the firm's Houzz profile. It is invisible on the actual homepage.

The About page appears empty or broken — it exists in navigation but has no accessible content, and the firm's philosophy statement (which eloquently covers custom design, historic restoration, residential-to-institutional range, and pioneering sustainable design) lives only on third-party platforms. There is **no contact form anywhere on the site** — only an email address and phone number. No services page exists to explain what the firm offers or how engagement works. No testimonials appear despite the firm's 30-year track record. No awards or press section showcases the AIA NH recognition, HGTV appearances, or magazine features. Portfolio listing pages show only images and names — no locations, dates, scope descriptions, or project types. Heading hierarchy is incorrect (project titles use H5 instead of H1), image alt text consists of raw filenames like "Vanderwarker_MCD5968.jpg," and Cindy Schartman's title reads "Senior Associate" on the website despite her promotion to Partner.

## How 14 benchmark firms set the standard

To contextualize ASA's current position, this analysis examined six major firms (Snøhetta, BIG, Zaha Hadid Architects, Studio Gang, Foster + Partners, Heatherwick Studio) and eight small-to-mid-sized practices (Olson Kundig, Selldorf Architects, Andersson/Wise, Ayers Saint Gross, Minale + Mann, Vita Architecture, Shape Studio, Mohagen Hansen) recognized for outstanding web presence through awards from Awwwards, CSS Design Awards, Archiboo, and The Drum.

### Large firms demonstrate strategic digital identity

Every major firm uses its website as a brand manifesto, not just a portfolio viewer. **Snøhetta's 2023 redesign** is the most conceptually rigorous example: built around digital sustainability, the site scores 97/100 on Google Lighthouse, produces only 0.09g of CO₂ per page view, and uses a custom typeface ("Dovre Social") with a deliberate black-and-white-only palette. Its signature dithered images — compressed using 16-color palettes and Floyd-Steinberg dithering — transform a constraint into a visual identity. The navigation distills down to three words: People, Process, Projects.

**BIG's homepage** is the most radical departure from convention: a mosaic grid of 100+ colored thumbnail tiles where hovering reveals project images and clicking expands to full case studies. The entire site revolves around the work, with nested filtering by discipline and typology. **Studio Gang** takes the opposite approach, leading with social justice values and advocacy positions before showcasing architecture — a values-first strategy that attracts philosophically aligned clients. **Zaha Hadid Architects** frames its site as "the interactive archive" and leads with scale metrics (**950 projects, 44 countries, 400 staff**) to establish immediate authority. **Heatherwick Studio** uniquely organizes work into Buildings, Objects, Spaces, Master-plans, and Infrastructure, reflecting its cross-scale practice from Longchamp handbags to Google campuses.

### Small and mid-sized firms prove you don't need scale to compete

The most instructive benchmarks for ASA come from firms operating at a comparable scale. **Olson Kundig** (Seattle, ~300 staff) offers the gold standard for portfolio filtering: projects filterable by 13 sectors, 5 designers, and 4 geographic regions, with each sector landing page featuring a descriptive paragraph on the firm's approach. Its standout "Play" section hosts recorded interviews, field visits, and a speaker series — a multimedia content hub that drives engagement and SEO simultaneously.

**Andersson/Wise** (Austin, 16 architects) proves that a small firm can project immense sophistication with a four-item navigation (Studio, Projects, Press + Awards, Contact), portrait-oriented thumbnail grids, and Imgix-optimized images. **Ayers Saint Gross** (Baltimore, ~200 professionals) won the Archiboo Best User Experience Award through multi-dimensional project filtering by service, sector, and expertise, combined with an "Ideas" content marketing hub publishing original research like "Comparing Campuses 2025: Spaces for Belonging." **Mohagen Hansen** (Minnesota) is cited industry-wide for exemplary CTA strategy, with "View Our Work" buttons in navigation, context-specific prompts throughout, and clear industry-vertical navigation that lets prospects self-qualify.

**Selldorf Architects** offers the most philosophically relevant model for ASA: a boutique New York firm whose website is restrained, elegant, and precise — "the website IS the brand statement," with a black-and-white palette that lets colorful photography dominate. Its differentiator appears on-site: "Since its inception the firm's design ethos has been deeply rooted in the principles of humanism." This proves that a small firm's website can convey quiet authority without flash.

## Quick wins that can be implemented immediately

These changes require no redesign, no platform migration, and minimal cost. They address the most critical conversion gaps using what already exists.

**Write and publish a homepage hero statement.** Take the firm's existing copy from Houzz — "Andrew Sidford Architects wants to raise your expectations of what architecture can be" — and place it prominently above the fold with a supporting sentence about 30 years of practice, residential through institutional range, and sustainability leadership. This single change addresses the most critical gap on the site. Below it, add a "Schedule a Consultation" button linking to the contact page.

**Add a contact form to the Contact page.** Wix offers native form builders. Create a simple intake form with fields for name, email, phone, project type (residential/commercial dropdown), project description, and timeline. This alone could significantly increase lead volume — the current email-only approach creates unnecessary friction. Research from Hinge Institute confirms that reducing contact friction is the single highest-impact lead generation improvement for professional services firms.

**Fix the About page.** Populate it with the firm's philosophy statement, the 1% For the Planet founding story (2006 — ahead of the entire industry), the sustainability commitment, the design approach covering custom design through historic restoration, and the geographic reach including the NYC expansion. This content exists scattered across Houzz, press interviews, and Instagram posts. It needs to live on the firm's own site.

**Update outdated details.** Change the copyright to 2026. Update Cindy Schartman's title from "Senior Associate" to "Partner." Update the LinkedIn link from Andrew's personal profile to a company page. Remove or reposition the Instagram feed so a job posting is not the homepage's most prominent content.

**Add an Awards & Press section.** Create a simple page listing the AIA NH Award for Jimmy's Jazz & Blues Club, features in Boston Globe Magazine, Design New England, Northshore Magazine, and HGTV appearances. Add logos of these publications. This is pure social proof that currently exists nowhere on the site.

**Fix image alt text.** Replace filenames like "Vanderwarker_MCD5968.jpg" with descriptive text like "Jimmy's Jazz and Blues Club interior showing restored timber trusses and contemporary glass addition, Portsmouth NH." This improves both accessibility and SEO at no cost beyond time. Similarly, change project page H5 headings to H1 for proper SEO hierarchy.

## Medium-term improvements requiring planning and design work

These changes build on quick wins and require some design effort, content creation, or professional services, but can be accomplished within the existing Wix platform or with modest investment.

**Create a Services page explaining how ASA works.** Architecture firms that publish their process see higher conversion because they reduce the anxiety of the unknown — most residential clients have never hired an architect. Structure this as a step-by-step journey: Initial Consultation → Site Analysis → Schematic Design → Design Development → Construction Documents → Construction Administration. For each phase, include a one-paragraph description, expected timeline, and what the client receives. Use this page to differentiate between residential and commercial engagement models. Benchmark: Mohagen Hansen's service presentation includes clear deliverables per phase, paired with project examples from each service type.

**Transform portfolio listings into mini case studies.** Currently, the Commercial and Residential listing pages show only images and project names. Add to each listing: **location, project type, completion year, and a one-sentence description.** On individual project pages, expand the existing narrative format (which is strong on Jimmy's) to all projects using a consistent template: Client Challenge → Design Approach → Key Features → Outcome. Add project specifications (approximate size, project type, duration) and, where possible, a client testimonial. Benchmark: Olson Kundig provides each sector with a dedicated landing page describing the firm's philosophy in that area before showing projects.

**Develop a testimonials program.** Reach out to 5-10 past clients across residential and commercial work for written testimonials. Place the strongest 2-3 on the homepage, and pair others with their respective project pages. Video testimonials are even more powerful — a 60-second client interview filmed at a completed project creates compelling social proof. Display these near CTAs throughout the site.

**Build contextual CTAs into every page.** On project pages, add: "Considering a similar project? Let's discuss your vision" with a link to the contact form. On the Services page: "Ready to start? Schedule a free consultation." On the About page: "Meet with our team." Benchmark: Mohagen Hansen places CTAs in navigation, after content blocks, and following project carousels. The firm's overall CTA structure is cited industry-wide as best-in-class for architecture.

**Launch a simple blog targeting local SEO.** Start with one post per month addressing questions potential clients actually search for: "How Much Does a Home Renovation Cost in Massachusetts?", "What to Expect When Hiring an Architect on the North Shore," "Historic Home Renovation in Newburyport: Permits and Process." These long-tail keywords have low competition and high intent. Firms publishing regular blog content see **50% more organic traffic** than those without. Each post should end with a relevant CTA.

**Optimize for local SEO.** Claim and fully optimize the Google Business Profile with professional project photos, complete service descriptions, and a request-review process. Create location-specific landing pages for key service areas (Newburyport, North Shore MA, Greater Boston, New York City). Target keywords like "residential architect Newburyport MA" and "historic renovation architect North Shore Massachusetts." Ensure NAP (name, address, phone) consistency across all directories including Houzz, AIA Find an Architect, and Yelp.

**Create separate audience pathways.** Research shows residential homeowners and commercial clients have fundamentally different needs and decision processes. Residential clients respond to emotional imagery, process education, cost transparency, and before/after transformations. Commercial clients respond to technical case studies, team credentials, sustainability certifications, and project scale. Consider a homepage entry point that lets visitors self-select: "Residential Clients" and "Commercial & Institutional Clients," each leading to tailored content, projects, and CTAs. Benchmark: Mohagen Hansen's industry-vertical navigation lets prospects self-qualify immediately.

## Full redesign recommendations for maximum impact

These recommendations represent the highest-impact changes requiring significant planning, investment, and potentially a platform migration. They should be pursued when the firm is ready for a comprehensive digital transformation.

### Platform migration from Wix to a professional CMS

Wix served its purpose but now limits ASA's growth. The template-driven approach constrains custom design, gallery presentation, and performance optimization. The recommended migration path is to **Webflow** — the platform of choice for architecture firms cited across industry sources, including Mohagen Hansen's website (designed by VnzoAEC, a specialist AEC web design firm). Webflow offers code-free design flexibility, built-in CMS for projects and blog content, excellent SEO tools, fast hosting on AWS, and the ability for the firm to update content without a developer. Alternative: **Craft CMS** (used by Snøhetta) offers maximum flexibility but requires developer support for updates.

### Visual identity that mirrors the architecture

ASA's website should feel like walking into one of the firm's spaces — purposeful, light-filled, and material-rich. The redesign should adopt a **restrained palette of black, white, and warm greys** inspired by Selldorf Architects' approach, where a monochromatic interface lets colorful project photography dominate. Typography should use one refined sans-serif family (or consider licensing a distinctive typeface as Snøhetta did with Dovre Social) at most two weights. Whitespace should be generous and intentional, framing photography like a gallery wall. The overall aesthetic should communicate the firm's range — from the contemporary glass addition at Jimmy's to the meticulous Federal-period restorations — without defaulting to either extreme.

Consider a **homepage hero strategy** using a full-bleed rotating image or short video loop of 3-4 signature projects with the firm's tagline overlaid. Below: a concise firm description, 3-4 featured projects as large image cards, a testimonial block, an awards/press logo bar, and a final CTA. Benchmark: Foster + Partners opens with a clear positioning statement — "We are a global studio for sustainable architecture, urbanism, engineering and design, founded by Norman Foster in 1967" — then lets featured projects and news build the narrative.

### Portfolio restructured as an immersive experience

Redesign the portfolio around three principles borrowed from the best firms analyzed. First, **filterable navigation**: allow visitors to filter by project type (residential, commercial, institutional, cultural), by service (new construction, renovation, historic restoration, sustainable design), and potentially by geography as the firm expands. Benchmark: Olson Kundig's triple-axis filtering by 13 sectors, 5 designers, and 4 regions. Second, **case study depth**: each project page should follow a consistent narrative arc — Context → Challenge → Design Approach → Solution → Impact — with professional photography, process documentation (sketches, drawings already present for Jimmy's), project specifications, and a client testimonial. Third, **visual immersion**: use full-bleed images with minimal chrome, implement a lightbox for high-resolution viewing, and consider video walkthroughs for signature projects. The Before & After section is a genuine differentiator — invest in **interactive before/after sliders** that let visitors drag to compare, replacing static side-by-side images.

Aim for **8-12 curated hero projects** on the main portfolio page with the full library accessible through filtering. Lead with the strongest work. Each project card on the listing page should show: hero image, project name, location, project type, and a one-line description.

### Content ecosystem for sustained lead generation

Build a content marketing engine with three tiers. The first tier is a **project story library** — every significant project becomes a blog post telling its story in 800-1,200 words with SEO-optimized titles like "Transforming a 1905 YMCA into Portsmouth's Premier Jazz Club." The second tier is **educational content** targeting client search queries: renovation budgeting guides, "what to expect" process articles, local building regulation explainers, and design trend pieces. The third tier is **thought leadership** content positioning Andrew Sidford and Cindy Schartman as experts: sustainability in New England architecture, balancing historic preservation with contemporary design, the case for 1% For the Planet in the built environment. Benchmark: Ayers Saint Gross's "Ideas" hub publishes original research that drives both SEO and brand authority.

Implement a **lead magnet system**: create a downloadable "Homeowner's Guide to Working with an Architect" (for residential clients) and a "Project Planning Checklist for Commercial Clients" (for institutional/commercial prospects). Gate these behind a simple email capture form. One firm in the research reported a **340% increase in qualified leads** after implementing an interactive design style quiz as a lead magnet.

### Sustainable web design as brand extension

Given ASA's pioneering sustainability commitment (first architecture firm to join 1% For the Planet in 2006), the redesigned website should itself be sustainably designed. Follow Snøhetta's model: optimize all images aggressively, minimize JavaScript, use green hosting, and publish the site's carbon footprint score. This turns a technical choice into a brand statement and creates PR opportunities. Test the site with the Website Carbon Calculator and aim for an A+ rating. This aligns the digital presence with the firm's core values in a way no competitor in their market size does.

## Implementation roadmap and expected impact

**Phase 1 — Quick wins (weeks 1-4, minimal cost):** Homepage hero text and CTA button, contact form, About page content, copyright and title updates, alt text and heading fixes, Awards & Press page. Expected impact: measurable increase in contact form submissions from day one; improved first impressions reducing bounce rate.

**Phase 2 — Medium-term improvements (months 2-4, moderate investment):** Services/process page, portfolio listing enhancements, testimonial collection and placement, contextual CTAs, blog launch with first 4 posts, Google Business Profile optimization, local SEO landing pages. Expected impact: improved search rankings for local architecture keywords within 3-6 months; structured lead capture enabling follow-up nurturing; distinct pathways for residential and commercial prospects.

**Phase 3 — Full redesign (months 4-8, significant investment):** Platform migration to Webflow, new visual identity system, restructured portfolio with filtering and case study format, interactive before/after features, content marketing engine, lead magnet development, sustainable web design implementation. Expected impact: a website that matches the caliber of the firm's 30-year body of work and positions ASA competitively as it expands into New York City.

The fundamental insight from this analysis is that **ASA's problem is not its work — it's that the work is invisible.** The firm has award-winning projects, national press features, a pioneering sustainability story, a newly promoted Partner, an NYC expansion, and 30 years of client relationships. None of this is being leveraged online. The firms that win in digital — from Selldorf's quiet elegance to Mohagen Hansen's conversion-optimized approach — understand that a website isn't a brochure. It's the front door to the practice. Andrew Sidford Architects deserves a front door that matches what's inside.
